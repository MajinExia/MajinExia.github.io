Background

After studying the principles, and heuristics of Interface Design, FurEverHome was created in a group of four as a platform to support new and prospective rescued dog owners in their journey with their beloved furry friends. Utilizing Figma over 6 weeks, I was primarily responsible for user research and testing, creating different iterations and features of the UI as well as prototyping a fully working prototype of one of our experience flows in the application.

Initial Research

The issue at hand

Approaching this project, my teammates and I first sought out research and journal articles, focusing on secondary research to familiarise ourselves with this domain and figure out if this is a domain we could potentially intervene and address with design. These articles pointed to resistance among potential and newly rescued dog owners with The Zebra’s survey noting 60% of adopted pets are returned or given away after 6 months, citing concerns about potential health risks and a lack of knowledge on how to effectively care for rescued pets.

"42.8% of pet owners favors shelter or rescue adoption for the chance to give second chances, however, without proper guidance and support, they may face challenges in ensuring successful and lasting adoptions"

— The Zebra’s 2023 survey with 1500 participants

User Interviews and Persona

To gain a better understanding of how pet owners and pet shelters view their experience with rescued dogs and the difference between them and buying from breeders, we conducted user interviews with 7 participants, each being 30 minutes long on both Zoom and in person, with 4 people who adopted from breeders, 2 who adopted rescues and a volunteer from the SPCA, a local animal shelters. From these interviews, we were able to synthesize the findings into insights and created 3 user personas of each of our potential end users with our primary user being a newly rescued pet owner.

With our primary target audience in mind, we created a user journey map to help the team further understand the different touchpoints and emotions a newly rescued pet owner might have to guide our design decisions in our low-fidelity wireframes our choice of colors in our app's style guide.

Style Guide

Low to High Fidelity Wireframes

When wireframing the application in grey box form, as a team, we all took part in coming up with different designs and ideas. When it came time to fully flesh out the screens, we diverged with each person taking a different user flow and having a meeting after each day to discuss our progress and address encountered issues collaboratively. Here I was mainly in charge of wireframing and prototyping the secondary user flow for potential users while providing support and feedback for the primary and the volunteer flow.

Competitive Analysis

When iterating on what how FurEverHome could differentiate itself, the team conducted a competitive analysis with each of us taking on a different dog app, using them for a week and then coming back as a team to discuss. One thing that we noticed while researching was that apart from encompassing a wider selection of features than other pet dog apps, FurEverHome truly stands out as the only application on the market (from our research) that focuses solely on rescue dogs.

User Testing and Addressing Issues

To gain a better understanding of how users would approach Fureverhome, the team conducted in-person user testing with 8 people consisting of 2 primary users, 4 secondary users and 2 volunteers, asking them to perform a setlist of different tasks utilising observation and the think-aloud method in combination with questionnaires.

However, after conducting these interviews, it was pointed out that while the features were well throught out, the prototype at that time still had issues with usability in parts of the UI and copywriting in the volunteer flow.

"The mood tracker feature alone makes me want to use the app"

— Primary user test participant

"The copywriting for the search bar on the home screen here needs to hint what it is for since I don’t really know if this is for resources or for (the) inbox"

— Volunteer user test participant

To optimize our project given our project timeline, I suggested to the team that we split the issues our participants encountered into high and low priorities fixes basing on how our participant found to be the biggest obstacle for them to use the application.

Introducing FurEverHome

Aiming to support both new and current rescued dog owners through working alongside local shelters and organizations such as the BC SPCA, we came up with "FurEverHome", the first app of its kind that focuses on making sure every rescued dog receives the love, care, and attention they deserve. Breaking Down The Main Features

Tailored Experience

Offering a personalized experience tailored to the specific needs of each individual, depending on whether they are a current pet owner, future pet owner, or volunteer with different layouts and functions made available for each group, further separated by the main color theme.

Ask A Volunteer

Users can explore posts contributed by other users and volunteers, providing a rich source of information and insights. Active participation is encouraged, as users can create their own posts to ask questions and seek advice from others in the community. Upon reaching 1000 PawPoints, which are measured based on the helpfulness of their posts as voted by other users, they will have the opportunity to earn a volunteer badge for their invaluable contributions to the community, offering them a way to give back to the community.

Curated Resources

Through working with local shelters such as the BC SPCA, the Resources tab includes a library of articles and videos made to help guide pet owner throughout their journey with their furry friend.

Reflection

As my first real UI/UX project that I have done in my academic career, I’m really proud of what my teammates and I were able to achieve, gaining both praise from fellow classmates and the teaching team during our final showcase. I was able to learn how to efficiently prototype a fully functional prototype application using Figma's components.

I've also gained a better understanding of the importance of qualitative research and its differences from quantitative and secondary research. I also learned how to create interview questions that don't lead the interviewee and encourage them to provide detailed and natural responses. Furthermore, I gotten more experience of collaborating within a team when developing a mobile application, especially keeping each other informed, and most importantly working together to overcome challenges we encountered basing our decisions on our research and user’s insights.
